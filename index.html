The hypothesis
The onboarding team delivers structured, expert-guided journeys. They work.
The hypothesis is that the product can do a much better job at its own part — and in doing so, make both the customer and the onboarding team more successful.
✅
The three-goal structure is a great framework for bundling expert knowledge into a deliverable journey — it gives the onboarding team a clear, reliable way to guide customers through complexity. This is not what this initiative challenges.
🪞
The in-product onboarding experience mirrors that framework — which was a reasonable starting point. But the product has a capability the OM doesn't: it can read the workspace in real time and know exactly where the customer is, what they've done, and what they need next.
🚀
If the product uses that capability — surfacing the right knowledge at the exact moment it's needed, scoped to the customer's specific goal — it can remove the routine from the onboarding team's plate and let them focus on what humans do best: complex guidance, architectural decisions, stakeholder alignment.
💡
This is not about replacing the onboarding team. It is about giving the product a voice in the customer's journey — one that is always on, always current, and never makes the customer wait. That combination is better for everyone.
The opportunity
Every customer is ready to move at their own pace — we should let them
📅
The OM model is optimised for structured delivery
The three-goal model is designed for human delivery — an expert pacing knowledge across sessions. It is the right structure for that job. But a product experience bound to the same cadence misses what the product is uniquely good at.
📚
The product can bridge the gap the OM bridges today
LeanIX has extensive documentation. In the current setup, the OM bridges the gap between the docs and the customer's actual situation. The product could do this automatically — surfacing only what is relevant, exactly when it is needed, without the customer having to ask.
🔁
We have a starting point — and a clear lesson from it
The Digital Onboarding Guide was the first attempt at self-paced onboarding. The hypothesis: it was too detached from what was actually happening in the workspace, and it tried to deliver knowledge in large bundles rather than at the moment it was needed.
💡
The change: just-in-time, goal-scoped knowledge
Instead of a knowledge bundle, deliver only the one piece of documentation that unlocks the next step — distilled to 5 steps, linked to the customer's specific goal. The product reads workspace state and decides what to surface, when.

is not building something new
The Data Quality Insights widget already exists, already reads workspace state, already surfaces gaps. The engineering ask is two additions: intent-scoping of the cards, and a guide trigger on each one.

That turns a passive scorecard into an active onboarding layer — without a new component, without AI, without a new nav surface.

The AI layer
The guide gets the customer ready for AI
The core experience requires zero AI. But every intent is designed so that once the customer has their data, the EA Assistant becomes dramatically more useful — and the value of unlocking it is immediately visible.

Next steps
Two questions to answer
The prototype is done. The ask is to move from prototype to evidence.
1
Can we make it available on a subset of new customers?
The pilot runs today as a browser injection on a demo workspace. The question is whether we can deploy the same experience — or a productized version of it — on a small cohort of real new customers during their first 90 days. This requires alignment on the right deployment path: native product integration, lightweight SDK, or controlled rollout.
2
Does it create value?
Measure against two signals: does the customer reach their first target report faster than the current average? And does the OM spend less time on knowledge delivery in the first 4 weeks? One cohort of 5–10 new customers is enough to know. One session of real use beats ten sessions of internal review.
Bootstrap → prototype → iterate. The prototype is done.
